Vector Equation of a Straight Line
The vector equation of a line passing through point A (with position vector \(\vec{a}\))
and parallel to a non-zero vector \(\vec{d}\) is
$$ \vec{p} = \vec{a} + t\vec{d} $$
Let O be the origin. The position vector of point A is \(\vec{OA} = \vec{a}\). Let P be any point on the line, with position vector \(\vec{OP} = \vec{p}\).
Since the line passes through A and is parallel to \(\vec{d}\), the vector \(\vec{AP}\) must be parallel to \(\vec{d}\). Therefore, \(\vec{AP} = t\vec{d}\) for some real scalar \(t\).
From the triangle law of vector addition (or by considering vector paths), we have: $$ \vec{OP} = \vec{OA} + \vec{AP} $$ Substituting the known vectors: $$ \vec{p} = \vec{a} + t\vec{d} $$ This is the vector equation of the line, where \(t\) is a parameter. As \(t\) varies over all real numbers, the point P traces out the entire line.
