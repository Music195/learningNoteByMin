Vector Equation of a Line Through Two Distinct Points
The vector equation of a line AB passing through two distinct points A(\(\vec{a}\)) and B(\(\vec{b}\)) is:
1. $$ \vec{p} = (1-t)\vec{a} + t\vec{b} $$
2. $$ \vec{p} = s\vec{a} + t\vec{b} \quad (s+t=1) $$
Let O be the origin. The position vectors of points A and B are \(\vec{OA} = \vec{a}\) and \(\vec{OB} = \vec{b}\) respectively. Let P be any point on the line passing through A and B, with position vector \(\vec{OP} = \vec{p}\).
The vector \(\vec{AP}\) is parallel to the vector \(\vec{AB}\). Therefore, \(\vec{AP} = t \vec{AB}\) for some real scalar \(t\).
We know that \(\vec{AB} = \vec{OB} - \vec{OA} = \vec{b} - \vec{a}\). So, \(\vec{AP} = t(\vec{b} - \vec{a})\).
Using vector addition, \(\vec{OP} = \vec{OA} + \vec{AP}\). Substituting the expressions, we get: $$ \vec{p} = \vec{a} + t(\vec{b} - \vec{a}) $$ $$ \vec{p} = \vec{a} + t\vec{b} - t\vec{a} $$ $$ \vec{p} = (1-t)\vec{a} + t\vec{b} $$ This is the first form of the equation.

For the second form, let \(s = 1-t\). Then \(s+t = (1-t)+t = 1\). Substituting \(s\) into the first equation: $$ \vec{p} = s\vec{a} + t\vec{b}, \quad \text{where } s+t=1 $$
ðŸ’¡ In vector equation 2, \(\vec{p} = s\vec{a} + t\vec{b}\) with \(s+t=1\):
  • If \(s \ge 0\) and \(t \ge 0\), then point P lies on the line segment AB (including endpoints A and B). This is because \(s=1-t \ge 0 \Rightarrow t \le 1\), and \(t \ge 0\), so \(0 \le t \le 1\).
    When \(t=0\), \(s=1\), \(\vec{p} = \vec{a}\) (Point A).
    When \(t=1\), \(s=0\), \(\vec{p} = \vec{b}\) (Point B).
    When \(0 < t < 1\), P is between A and B.
  • If \(t < 0\), then \(s = 1-t > 1\). Point P lies on the line extended beyond A (on the side opposite to B).
  • If \(t > 1\), then \(s = 1-t < 0\). Point P lies on the line extended beyond B (on the side opposite to A).
The condition for P to be on the line segment AB is: $$ \vec{p} = s\vec{a} + t\vec{b}, \quad s+t=1, \quad s \ge 0, \quad t \ge 0 $$
