Magnitude of a Vector
If \(\vec{a} = (a_1, a_2, a_3)\), then its magnitude is:
$$ |\vec{a}| = \sqrt{\textcolor{red}{a_1^2 + a_2^2 + a_3^2}} $$
Understanding Vector Magnitude:
The magnitude (or length) of a vector \(\vec{a} = (a_1, a_2, a_3)\) represents the distance from the origin (0,0,0) to the point (a₁, a₂, a₃) in 3D space.

This is a direct application of the Pythagorean theorem extended to three dimensions.
  • First, consider the projection of the vector onto the xy-plane. This projection forms a right triangle with sides \(a_1\) and \(a_2\), and its length is \(\sqrt{a_1^2 + a_2^2}\).
  • Then, this projected length and the z-component \(a_3\) form another right triangle, where the magnitude of \(\vec{a}\) is the hypotenuse.
Thus, \(|\vec{a}|^2 = (\sqrt{a_1^2 + a_2^2})^2 + a_3^2 = a_1^2 + a_2^2 + a_3^2\).
Taking the square root gives the formula: \(|\vec{a}| = \sqrt{a_1^2 + a_2^2 + a_3^2}\).
💡 Key Insights:
  • The magnitude of a vector is always a non-negative scalar value.
  • It is synonymous with the length of the vector or the norm of the vector (specifically, the Euclidean norm or L2 norm).
  • If \(\vec{a} = (a_1, a_2)\) in 2D, its magnitude is \(|\vec{a}| = \sqrt{a_1^2 + a_2^2}\). The 3D formula is a natural extension.
  • The concept generalizes to n-dimensional space: for \(\vec{x} = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)\), the magnitude is \(|\vec{x}| = \sqrt{x_1^2 + x_2^2 + \ldots + x_n^2}\).
  • A vector with magnitude 1 is called a unit vector.
